François Bertrand's political fight, his Holy Grail, the cause to which he had devoted his entire life, was to eliminate gentrification from Lower-City Québec.

Or so he hammered home at each of his public appearances, with increasing insistence as the electoral campaign progressed. Soon, every ear in Québec, young or old, large or small, would have finally heard him and his incandescent breath — a politician whose career would forever change the face of the world (for the better, needless to say).

The Great François Bertrand was renowned in his party's activist circles for his invincibility in debate, his formidable rhetoric, and his calm, unshakeable temperament — even when opponents reminded him that, for a proud fighter against gentrification, it was somewhat ironic that he lived at the top of the most luxurious condo tower in his district, a district that happened to be considered the most disadvantaged in the Capitale-Nationale. To which Bertrand would respond: "Sometimes, to address certain issues, it is useful to gain perspective." (Applause.)

If the mystery of his first-place standing in the polls remained unsolved for many analysts, the Legendary François Bertrand was hardly surprised: in his view, citizens had "instinct." He repeated to anyone willing to listen (in his view, everyone) that debates were not so important for forming an opinion, that the bearing of Great Men was enough. What the Succulent François Bertrand did not know, however, was that three days before the election, magic would appear on Earth and that, for the first time in his life, our valiant hero would not be able to control everything.

It was the end of summer. Soon the sun would stop setting at 11 p.m. and temperatures would drop from 45°C to 30°C. Meteorologists were forecasting a cool autumn, with average temperatures around 27°C, and only one or two tornadoes for October. Finally, a little normalcy, François thought behind his steering wheel, the radio in his ears, waiting for the weather segment to end so they could talk about him again. What an idea, calling elections in the middle of summer! I'm sick of sweating like a pig. At least, the advantage of being a man of the left is that you can trade suits and ties for an open-collar shirt. You can also roll your sleeves up to your elbows — casual-looking. That works. He was actually wondering why certain magazines hadn't contacted him yet for their cover; usually the relaxed politician angle plays well. The ladies like that.

They didn't talk about him after the segment. For a reason that escaped him, they chose to address the housing crisis rather than solicit the opinions of men ready to fix it. Ideas are all well and good, but you need people to embody them. And to top it off, they preferred to ask the opinion of the sitting MNA for Taschereau. As if she knows what she's talking about! The situation has never been worse than since she took that seat. Roll on September 5th. It was too much — he preferred to turn off the radio.

After all, he was about to arrive at the press conference on gender parity in politics.
