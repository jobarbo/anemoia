## The Lore

It is in Québec, that peculiar northern city, that we must trace the origin of vertical cities.

It all began in year 12 before the Ecomobilist Revolution (-12 REM), during yet another city council meeting on the construction of a seventh link between the ancient Haut-Lieu of Lévis and Québec (also known, in the oldest archives, as "the accent of America"). Citizens, long divided over questions of mobility, inter-urbanism, and ecology, found common cause when Mayors Montpetit and Taschereau revealed to their respective populations that the bridge in question would serve only to support gas pipelines — eighteen of them, according to the records, stacked one upon the other. A betrayal that would bring down the municipal, provincial, and federal leaders of the era. All were imprisoned in the Complexe G by a coalition of revolutionary militias. The bridge's construction, already well underway, would never reach completion: its ruins can still be glimpsed, on mornings without smoke, from the far bank.

In the wake of this unprecedented seizure of power by the people, citizens had the chance to build a new society — more just, more prosperous, more democratic.

But none of that came to pass.

These are the circumstances that gave rise to the "Anémoia" era; a time everyone idealizes today without ever having lived through it. All that remains of that period in history is accessible through the journal logs of the old Archivist's registrarium. Without him, we could never have documented what truly happened.
