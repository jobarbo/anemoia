Ginette had just finished her eighteenth hour of work.

Her ankles swollen and her back twisted, she hauled her twentieth bag of cans of the day over her shoulder. Monday through Saturday, the woman the neighborhood called "the aluminum hunter" kept the same routine: she rose before dawn, pulled on her wool socks, her purple tracksuit jacket, her washed-out jeans, ate a bowl of Froot Loops, then headed out with a roll of blue plastic bags tucked under her arm. At noon, when the sun was frying the city's dusty districts, she'd go home to drop off her coat. She'd eat a cretons sandwich and let her water-logged ankles drain while she waited for the temperature to drop a little. Her living room reeked of beer, but over time her nose had made its peace with the smell: her daily existence consisted of rummaging through every trash can in all of Cité-Limoilou-Beach in search of returnable cans, often dripping with the nectar of the neighborhood's microbreweries. For fifteen years, her route had remained the same, stretching from Haute-City all the way down to Basse-City, from South Limoilou Beach to the gentle Saint-Rock'n'roll.
Ginette didn't talk much: she had always lived alone and, since the death of Yum Yum, the urge to adopt an animal had never returned.

— They're looking for people at the SPCA to foster cats while they find them families, why don't you go? her upstairs neighbor would say from time to time, to which Ginette never knew what to answer.

— I'm not home during the day, how do you expect me to look after a kitten. They need a mother who's there, someone with the schedule for it.

Aside from her neighbor, Ginette spoke to no one, except the convenience store clerks who set cans aside for her and old Ronald, who was competing with her in the neighborhood with his own returns business. If it weren't for that son of a bitch, I could sleep a little later in the morning.

Her legs aching, her heels covered in blisters, sighing easily, she was approaching the Desbiens convenience store, the only one still open after midnight. The place lit the whole street with its asylum-white neon. The closer Ginette got, the more the constant electric hum scraped at the back of her ears. The cool night air threaded through the folds of her worn clothing, probing for any possible gap. Someone's going to have to explain to me how it can be so scorching during the day and this goddamn cold at night. Honestly — is it just me, or does the middle ground not exist anymore?

As usual, the owner sighed when she saw her dragging her bag of cans, and as usual, she made no move to help. Not that it mattered — Ginette knew the drill: she had to throw the bag down below the steps, that final burden marking the end of her day. Knowing Ginette always brought the same number of cans, Madame Desbiens had prepared her change in advance, a small stack of coins set at the end of the counter near the door. She wished her good evening without looking up from her television. If I were her, I'd be worn out. The days must feel long, watching the news on repeat.

Heading out, Ginette crossed paths with old Ronald, arriving with his haul. They exchanged a nod, at once respectful of each other's enterprise and wary that the other might finally get the upper hand. Yeah, that's it, Ronald, don't bother wishing me a good evening. Doesn't matter anyway. I couldn't give a damn about you.

Walking down the middle of the street, slowly but surely, Ginette let her thoughts drift outward, searching for something — anything — that might, for once, pull her from her routine.
That evening, nothing came.
